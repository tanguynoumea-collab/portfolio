## Context

This conceptual study explores a fifteen-storey ecological residential tower designed for a dense urban block. A personal research project, it let me test my BIM tools against an ambitious vertical typology: greened façades, natural ventilation, and a mix of dwellings. The goal was to produce a credible concept rather than a purely spectacular image.

## Challenge

Designing for height imposes strong structural and climatic constraints. The challenge was to reconcile a regular structural grid with staggered planted balconies while guaranteeing enough daylight for the lower dwellings. The complex façade geometry also had to remain workable in BIM, not frozen in a simple form model.

## Process

I modelled the structural grid and cores in Revit, then exported the façade skin to Rhino to explore balcony variations through a parametric logic. The two models were synchronised to keep the structure and envelope consistent.

<Callout variant="info" title="Revit / Rhino round-trip">
  The parametric façade is designed in Rhino then reimported as an adaptive family in Revit — the geometry stays driven by parameters rather than frozen into dead mesh.
</Callout>

I ran a seasonal shadow study to validate daylight, adjusting the balcony offset accordingly. Twinmotion renders were used to communicate the atmosphere of the project: controlled vegetation, natural light, and continuity with the surrounding urban fabric.

## Outcome

The study results in a coherent concept where structure, façade, and climatic performance work together. The parametric workflow made it possible to test a dozen façade variations in a few hours, where manual modelling would have taken several days. This project remains a showcase of my ability to carry a complete architectural reflection, from the structural stance to the final render.
